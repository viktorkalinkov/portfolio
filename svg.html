The assignment

Create a concept that would help get the neighbourhood to exercise and do activities together by researching why/how they want to participate. And if they do not want to, our goal is to find out why and further improve the concept.


For the approach we are going to be utilizing the waterfall method. We will divide the project into 5 consequential sprints. In the problem definition phase, we will work with the stakeholders to understand the current challenges and define the project goals and objectives. We will then create a Product Backlog, which will include all the tasks that need to be completed to achieve the project goals, using Trello During the sprint planning phase, we will review the Product Backlog and select the tasks that will be completed during the upcoming sprint. The length of each sprint will be two weeks, and we will hold a Sprint Planning Meeting at the beginning of each sprint to set priorities and establish sprint goals.


Goal of the project

This project aims to promote physical activity and convert extrinsic motivation into intrinsic sports motivation in Eindhoven's neighbourhoods, with a focus on the Stratum region, which is the city's most diverse area. The project seeks to gather both quantitative and qualitative data to gain insights into how public spaces can be designed to encourage exercise activities and motivate residents to become more vital. By bringing people together to exercise and understanding the factors that motivate or discourage participation, the project aims to provide the municipality of Eindhoven with a concept that promotes physical activity and social interaction.


Goal of the municipality of Eindhoven:

  • To determine policy for sports and exercise in the neighborhoods of Eindhoven through data.
  • To encourage people to exercise regularly and maintain their activity levels, we need to turn motivation deriving from the community into personal motivation for sports.

Problem:

  • Stratum is a diverse region in Eindhoven with some of its parts being with lower vitality than other neighborhoods.
  • Created survey, but no info on the demographic that filled it (age, sex, etc.)

Risks:

When mapping a neighborhood with the intent to relocate residents, there are several risks that must be considered.


  • Privacy risk: The project may include the collection and sharing of data on people's movements and habits, raising privacy issues. It is critical to ensure that the project complies with applicable data privacy laws and regulations.
  • Community buy-in: If the initiative does not have community support, its efficacy may be limited. To ensure that the project is well-received, it is critical to interact with the community and stakeholders early in the process.
  • Bias and prejudice: The data gathered may reflect biases or discrimination in the community, perpetuating disparities. It is critical to guarantee that data is collected and processed objectively.

1. What is the recommended method for delivering the outcomes to the various target groups?


Resources:

  • "Communicating EU research and innovation guidance for project participants " - The EU Framework Programme for Research and Innovation

Delivering outcomes to different target groups requires a flexible approach that depends on the specific context and audience. To ensure effective delivery of outcomes, it is crucial to understand your audience by considering their needs, interests, and preferences. Tailoring your delivery methods based on factors such as age, gender, education level, and cultural background can lead to better engagement and increased likelihood of positive results.


Another important factor is the use of multiple channels to reach a wider audience. By taking the time to tailor your delivery methods and seek feedback, you can ensure that your outcomes are effectively delivered and understood, ultimately leading to better results.


2. How can we use a virtual personal assistant?


Resources:

  • "Digital personal assistants are smart ways for assistive technology to aid the health and wellbeing of patients and carers" - Authors: Gayathri Victoria Balasubramanian, Paul Beaney, Ruth Chambers

  • "The rise of Virtual Personal Assistants" - Authors: Vincent Bonneau, IDATE and Laurent Probst, Virginie Lefebvre, PwC

Virtual personal assistants (VPAs) are poised to revolutionize both individual and organizational vitality by leveraging cutting-edge AI and machine learning technologies. VPAs can offer personalized recommendations and support to meet each user's unique needs, promoting their wellness and community growth.


VPAs offer a wide range of benefits to users. They can help manage schedules and set reminders for exercise routines or other wellness-related activities based on the user's profile. VPAs can also assist in tracking users' health and wellness by monitoring exercise routines, reminding them to take medications, and even tracking nutrition. They can also facilitate municipal and public communications, including scheduling assistance, reminders, and coordinating with volunteers. Additionally, VPAs can identify and track the needs of the community and individuals, highlighting where additional resources may be needed. Furthermore, based on the user's health, VPAs can offer mental health support by recommending local resources, guided meditations, and setting reminders for self-care practices.


In summary, VPAs are a versatile tool that can be tailored to meet the needs and preferences of users to promote individual and community growth. By providing personalized assistance for day-to-day needs, VPAs can help individuals and communities thrive.


3. How do we define co-operation, participation, ownership?


Resources:

  • "Definition of Participation" - Claridge, T., 2004. Designing social capital sensitive participation methodologies. Report, Social Capital Research, Brisbane, Australia.

  • "Community Participation"

  • "Socio-Technical Design and Social Networking Systems" - Authors: Laurence Claeys and Johan Criel

Co-operation: “Cooperatives are created by people who have a specific need and who are willing to work together to operate and organize a company that will meet that need.” In more comprehensible words: Cooperation refers to working together towards a common goal, where individuals or groups combine their efforts to achieve a shared objective.


Participation: “The myth that the affected population is too shocked and helpless to take responsibility for their own survival is superseded by the reality that on the contrary, many find new strength during an emergency” (Goyet, 1999). “Participation means the involvement of intended beneficiaries in the planning, design, implementation, and subsequent maintenance of the development intervention. It means that people are mobilized, manage resources, and make decisions that affect their lives” (Price and Mylius 1991) During our day and age there is an increase in expectations for participation and engagement in decision-making processes, especially when it comes to issues that affect people’s everyday lives. Participatory approaches, involve the community in the design and decision-making process. This can lead to a range of benefits, such as increased social cohesion, improved health and well-being, and greater sustainability. When residents are involved in the design process, they can provide valuable insights into their needs and desires, which can lead to living environments that are more tailored to their needs.


Ownership: In Europe, the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) governs data ownership when someone fills out a survey. While the organization that creates and conducts the survey usually owns the collected data, the GDPR sets strict guidelines on how personal data, including survey data, can be collected, stored, processed, and shared. Organizations must obtain explicit and informed consent from survey respondents to collect and process their personal data, and they must also take appropriate measures to ensure the security of the data. Survey respondents have the right to access, modify, erase, and restrict processing of their data. Organizations must respect these rights and respond promptly to requests from survey respondents regarding their personal data.
