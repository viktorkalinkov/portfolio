INTRODUCTION

InnoBeweegLab, a Dutch company specializing in innovative solutions for physical activity and wellness, recognizes the significance of a healthy and exercise-friendly environment highlighted by the upcoming environmental law. A crucial aspect of this vision is to provide suitable physical activities for all residents in the neighborhood. To achieve this goal, citizen participation plays a vital role in understanding their interests and preferences. InnoBeweegLab has developed the WijkBeweegKaart, a tool designed to gather information from local residents and foster community engagement. Unlike traditional methods such as surveys or neighborhood meetings, which often have limitations in terms of interaction, effectiveness, and timely results, InnoBeweegLab, in collaboration with the municipality of Eindhoven and Fontys through the Germ project, is implementing a different approach to address these challenges specifically in the Stratum district.

MAIN GOAL

The project has selected the Stratum region in Eindhoven due to its remarkable diversity in terms of social and cultural backgrounds. Within this region, specific areas exhibit low vitality, and simply relying on a resident survey is inadequate to fully comprehend the underlying factors. Consequently, the project endeavors to collect a comprehensive range of quantitative and qualitative data to acquire profound insights into effectively engaging local residents in the process of designing public spaces that prioritize exercise activities. The ultimate goal is to motivate residents to enhance their vitality and well-being.


Used method

For our project, we've decided to utilize the Waterfall Model. This means that we'll be following a step-by-step approach, moving through the phases in the exact order they're listed. Initially, our team will gather and carefully analyze all the requirements for the project. After that, we'll proceed to design the system, implementing it, and finally conducting thorough testing.
The beauty of the Waterfall Model lies in its structured and organized nature, which brings several benefits to our project. Each phase has a well-defined set of objectives and deliverables, allowing us to maintain a clear overview of the project's development and ensure everything is proceeding smoothly.

Time management

The project will follow a structured approach consisting of 5 sequential iterations. In the initial phase, we will gather valuable insights from stakeholders to identify the core problems. Based on this, a Product Backlog will be created using Trello. During sprint planning, tasks will be carefully selected to be completed within two-week intervals. Throughout each iteration, regular stand-up meetings and Sprint Review sessions will be held, incorporating Design Thinking methodologies on a weekly basis. In the final phase, we will focus on refining deliverables, conducting thorough testing and validation of the solution, and conducting an inclusive review with stakeholders to ensure the project objectives are achieved.

Research & Tools

In our project, we implemented the Development Oriented Triangulation (DOT) framework, which proved to be a robust research approach. This framework excels in organizing research questions into five distinct categories, facilitating a comprehensive exploration of our project's objectives. With its three levels, namely the "What," the "Why," and the "How" of the research, the DOT framework provided a structured and practical methodology that guided our investigation effectively. Additionally, we leveraged advanced research tools that enhanced our data collection, analysis, and interpretation capabilities. By combining the power of the DOT framework and these cutting-edge tools, we successfully gained valuable insights and achieved significant progress in our project.

FIRST SPRINT: REQUIREMENTS & PLANNING

Requirements & Planning


The main focus was on acquiring a comprehensive understanding of the project requirements and determining the desired outcomes. A crucial step in achieving these objectives was the development of a detailed project plan. To establish an effective plan, it was imperative to delve deeply into the assignment, clarify the situation, and identify the key research question.

The project's scope encompasses various components such as a research report, advice report, test report, prototypes, and proof of concept. However, it does not involve the inclusion of a database, back end, API connections, or other elements that are irrelevant to the research and development of the concept.

The ultimate deliverable of this project will be a concept aimed at fostering neighborhood engagement in exercise and group activities. This will be achieved through thorough research into the motivations and preferences of the community members regarding participation. Additionally, if individuals show reluctance to participate, the goal is to investigate the reasons behind it and further enhance the concept accordingly.

The research questions to be explored encompass a range of topics, including exercise motivation, targeted approaches for different groups, privacy and safety measures, virtual personal assistants, gamification, vitality benchmarking, cooperation dynamics, participation levels, and ownership aspects.

    To understand the target group and their needs, we conducted a vitality survey. I helped with defining what the questions will be for the survey. Unfortunatly, I was not able to attend the inteviewing. Hazem was accompanied by Plamen, they managed to interviewed approximately 15 individuals from the target group (people living in Stratum). The survey aimed to uncover why and how people move, as well as the motivating factors behind their physical activity. These insights will inform our concept development process.

While they have a desire to engage in physical activity, they are not primarily motivated by rewards or vouchers. They express a preference for group exercise but often face challenges related to time and finances.

    How Might We:


    Based on our research findings, we used the "How Might We" (HMW) method to explore ways to motivate people to be more active. The main question we focused on was: How can we inspire individuals to move?

    To address this, we answered related sub-questions and came up with ideas such as using a virtual assistant to provide personalized recommendations, creating a fitness app based on budget and goals, developing a platform for local event information, incorporating gamification, designing a system for group tracking and organization, offering personalized training options, and creating an app to track health benefits. These ideas aim to encourage and motivate individuals to engage in physical activity in a way that suits their preferences and goals.

    New Concept:


    We came up with the idea of a survey platform with gamification elements to enhance user engagement and increase response rates. Through this platform, users can create their own customized avatars, participate in surveys, suggest topics, and earn points to level up. The platform not only encourages active user involvement but also cultivates a sense of community through a ranking system based on points. The personalized avatars serve as interactive guides, making the user experience more enjoyable and immersive.

    To ensure efficient development, we created user stories outlining all the possible features of the platform. We then utilized the MoSCoW method to assess the importance of each user story and prioritize the implementation of features accordingly. This approach allowed us to streamline our development process and focus on delivering the most valuable and impactful features to our users.
